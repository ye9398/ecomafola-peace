# Pacific Islander Artisans We Love: The Makers Behind Ecomafola

There's a workshop behind a mango tree in Leulumoega where the sound of weaving starts before sunrise.

Afioga Mele has been up since 4:30. She's already stripped three bundles of pandanus leaf, split them thin with her thumbnail, and laid them out on the mat to dry. By the time the sun clears the ridge, her hands are moving on their own. They've been doing this since she was seven years old, sitting at her grandmother's feet on the same lava floor, learning which leaves bend and which ones break.

We met Mele two years ago during our first sourcing trip to Savai'i. She didn't speak much at first. Just kept weaving. Then she showed us a mat her grandmother made forty years ago — still intact, still beautiful. "I want mine to last that long," she said.

That's the standard here. Not speed. Not volume. Longevity.

At Ecomafola, we work with over 240 families across Samoa, Fiji, Tonga, and Vanuatu. Every single product you find on our site was made by someone we know by name. Someone whose kids we've met. Whose church we've sat in. Whose taro patch we've walked through.

This isn't a supply chain. It's a network of people.

## The Hands That Make What You Hold

### Afioga Mele — Master Weaver, Savai'i

Mele works out of her family fale in a village on the northwest coast of Savai'i. The pandanus she uses grows behind her house. Her daughter, Tia, is twelve and already better at splitting leaves than most adults.

Each coir mat takes Mele about five days from start to finish. That's stripping, drying, dyeing with natural bark, and weaving the pattern her grandmother taught her. She makes two to three mats a month on top of everything else she does — cooking, gardening, church choir on Sundays.

When we started buying from Mele, she was selling her mats at the Apia market for $8 each. We pay her $36 for the same mat. She cried the first time we told her our price. Not the dramatic kind. The quiet kind where you look away and wipe your cheek with the back of your hand.

Her house has a new tin roof now. Paid in cash.

### Tavita Ioane — Coconut Bowl Carver, Apia

Tavita's workshop is a corrugated iron shed behind his brother's mechanic shop in Apia. You'll hear the grinder before you see him.

He sources his coconut shells from a copra processor in Vaiusu — shells that would otherwise be burned as waste. Every week he picks up roughly 200 shells, culls the cracked ones, and starts the process of sanding, shaping, and polishing. Each bowl takes about three hours of active work, spread over two days because the oil finish needs time to cure.

Tavita started carving after a layoff from the tourism sector in 2020. He'd always carved for fun — small pieces for family, a few gifts. When the borders closed, he turned it into income. We carry about 15 of his designs, from small condiment bowls to large serving pieces.

At our price point, Tavita earns roughly $15 per bowl. He averages 30 to 40 bowls a month. That's $450 to $600 directly in his pocket — well above Samoa's average monthly income.

He bought his daughter a laptop for school last year. Said she needed it for her assignments. Then he winked and added, "So she can stop borrowing her cousin's."

### Lani Tuatagaloa — Shell Jewelry Maker, Fagali'i

Lani doesn't use fresh shells. That's a hard line for her.

Every shell in her jewelry comes from beach combing, storm wash-ups, or food waste from local restaurants in the Fagali'i area. She walks the same two-kilometer stretch of reef flat every morning with a bucket and a eye for what's already detached. No living creatures. No active reef harvesting.

Her most popular piece — the Loto necklace — uses tiny cowrie shells strung on hand-braided coconut fiber. Each one takes about 90 minutes. She makes 20 to 25 a week, sometimes more during the high season.

Lani runs a small collective of six women from her village. They meet twice a week at her place, shell through the morning's collection, and string together while the kids play in the yard. She pays each woman directly from her Ecomafola earnings. The group's combined monthly income from us runs between $800 and $1,200.

"We used to ask our husbands for money," Lani told us. "Now they ask us."

### Sione and the Leulumoega Coir Collective

Sione isn't a weaver. He's a fisherman who started making coir rope when the catch got thin. His village in Leulumoega has a long tradition of coir work — beating coconut husks, extracting the fibers, twisting them into cord. But the knowledge was fading. Younger generations weren't learning.

So Sione organized. He got together eight families, mostly men who worked in fishing or farming, and they started making coir products full-time. Mats, baskets, rope, decorative pieces. They use traditional techniques passed down through generations, but they've adapted the designs for modern homes.

The collective operates out of a shared fale near the coast. Everyone contributes raw materials. Everyone shares the income. It works because there's no single boss. Sione coordinates, but decisions are made together.

Ecomafola buys from the collective at a fixed rate that works out to about $20 per item on average. Last quarter alone, that put roughly $4,800 into the hands of those eight families.

## How the 60% Revenue Share Actually Works

Most fair trade claims give 10 to 20 percent back to makers. We give 60 percent of the retail price. Directly. No middlemen, no agent fees, no "processing costs" deducted before the artisan sees a dollar.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

- A $25 coconut bowl from Tavita means **$15 goes to Tavita**
- A $48 coir mat from Mele means **$28.80 goes to Mele**
- A $35 Loto necklace from Lani means **$21 goes to Lani**
- A $60 woven basket from the Leulumoega collective means **$36 goes to the collective**

The remaining 40 percent covers our actual costs. Shipping from the islands. Packaging that doesn't use plastic. Website hosting. Marketing so people actually find these products. And yes, a small margin that keeps Ecomafola running.

We don't pretend the other 40 percent is charity. It's the cost of getting Pacific-made products into your hands. But we also don't pretend that "fair trade" means a nice sticker and a vague promise. You should know exactly where your money goes.

## The Mafola Artisan Fund

Beyond the 60 percent revenue share, we set aside 5 percent of all sales into the Mafola Artisan Fund. It's not a marketing initiative. It's a real pool of money that goes to three things:

**Health.** Medical expenses are one of the biggest financial shocks for families in rural Pacific communities. The fund has covered dental work, prescription medications, and emergency transport to Apia hospital for artisan families. Last year alone, we helped 14 families with health costs totaling over $6,200.

**Micro-finance.** Some artisans need a small loan to buy materials, upgrade tools, or cover costs while waiting for a large order to ship. The fund provides interest-free loans — typically $100 to $500 — repaid over 3 to 6 months. We've issued 32 loans since starting the program. The repayment rate is 97 percent.

**Education.** Several artisan families have used the fund to cover school fees, uniforms, and supplies. We've supported 23 students so far. The average grant is $180 per student per term.

The fund is transparent. We share quarterly updates with all artisan partners so they know how much is available and how it's being allocated.

## Women-Led Cooperatives and What They Change

Most of the artisans we work with are women. That's not by design — it's just how Pacific craft traditions work. Weaving, shell work, fiber arts — these have historically been women's domains.

What's changed is the economic power that comes with selling beyond the village market.

When a woman in Savai'i earns $300 a month from her weaving, she doesn't spend it on herself. She pays school fees. She buys rice and tinned fish for the household. She contributes to the church fund. She saves for emergencies. She becomes the person others come to when they need help.

We've seen this pattern repeat across every cooperative we work with. Women who started making crafts as a side income are now the primary earners in their households. That shifts something fundamental in how decisions get made, whose voice carries weight, and what the next generation of girls sees as possible.

Lani's collective in Fagali'i started with three women. It now has fifteen. They've opened a joint bank account. They're planning to buy a shared workspace. They talk about expanding into export markets on their own terms.

None of that would have happened without a buyer willing to pay what the work is actually worth.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Where do Ecomafola's artisans live?**

Our artisan network spans 240+ families across four Pacific Island nations: Samoa (primarily Savai'i and Upolu), Fiji (Suva and surrounding villages), Tonga (Tongatapu), and Vanuatu (Efate and Santo). Most artisans work from their homes or shared community spaces.

**How much do Pacific Islander artisans earn from Ecomafola?**

We operate on a 60 percent revenue share model. For every product sold, 60 percent of the retail price goes directly to the artisan who made it. That's significantly higher than the 10 to 20 percent typical in conventional fair trade arrangements.

**How does Ecomafola verify fair trade practices?**

We visit every artisan partner in person. Our team travels to Samoa, Fiji, Tonga, and Vanuatu multiple times per year to check in on working conditions, review payments, and discuss pricing. We also maintain the Mafola Artisan Fund — a transparent pool sourced from 5 percent of all sales that goes toward health, education, and micro-finance for artisan families.

**Can I visit the artisan workshops?**

Yes. We organize annual artisan open days in Samoa, and we're happy to connect interested customers with our cooperative leaders. Most artisans welcome visitors — just reach out to us in advance so we can coordinate with the communities.

**What makes Ecomafola's handmade Pacific Islander products different from mass-produced alternatives?**

Every Ecomafola product is individually handmade using traditional techniques and natural, sustainably sourced materials. No two items are identical. Our artisans use pandanus, coconut shell, coconut fiber, and sustainably sourced shells — nothing synthetic, nothing factory-made. When you buy from us, you're supporting specific families and their communities, not a faceless manufacturing process.
